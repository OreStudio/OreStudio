* Summary

This dataset is a design document, not yet a populated data set: it
grounds the proposed Acme Bank holding-group structure in Barclays'
real corporate shape, maps that shape onto ORE Studio's current
=party=/=account= entity model, and records the gaps that block
populating it today. It is the analysis output of [[id:69E9CD42-EED4-403F-9331-AE369B8581B6][task: Analyse Acme
Bank corporate structure and current populatability]] in the
[[id:CB7351E9-8E7E-4BA4-9399-4EB22F6192D4][Commission Acme Bank]] story. The proposed structure below is the
blueprint for the actual seeding work in later tasks of that story.

* Scoping decisions made during this analysis

- *Acme owns everything.* The business units, portfolios, books, and
  report definitions currently in the system as example/test fixtures
  are ownerless — they don't hang off a real party. Rather than
  leaving them dangling, they should be re-parented under Acme Bank so
  there is a single coherent "fully populated" entity, not orphaned
  example data plus a separate party tree. This affects the *scope*
  of what Acme owns but not this task, which stays analysis-only.
- *Business unit/portfolio/book population is deferred.* =prime_origin=
  is mid-refactor of the DQ bundle-publish pipeline into a unified,
  data-driven "risk management dataset" mechanism (see [[id:F05B779F-769B-49D5-B369-D2AAC029B36D][DQ Bundle
  Publication Pipeline]]). Building bespoke business-unit/portfolio/book
  seeding now would be thrown away once that lands. This task instead
  focuses on what doesn't depend on that refactor: party hierarchy,
  LEIs, contacts, and staff accounts.
- *Realistic but not exhaustive.* Three offices — London, New York,
  Hong Kong — cover the follow-the-sun trading day for a global
  investment bank without ballooning headcount. A trading floor (Rates,
  Credit, FX desks) plus Middle Office and Market Risk support
  functions per region is enough to exercise RBAC across front/middle
  office without needing "the biggest company."
- *Staff accounts need two new capabilities*: locale-matched names
  (the office's country drives the name generator's locale) and a
  profile picture per account, loosely matched to the generated name's
  apparent gender/ethnicity via the =external/facestudio/faces=
  dataset. Both are gaps in the current schema (see below).
- *A hardcoded company structure, generated with a script.* The
  org chart itself (offices, desks, headcounts, role mix) is
  hand-authored — it needs a human's judgement about what's a
  "sensible" bank shape. The *data* for each of those slots (names,
  LEIs, emails, addresses, pictures) is what a Python generator (Faker
  or equivalent) fills in, keyed by locale, so re-running it is
  repeatable/idempotent rather than a one-off manual exercise.

* Design principle: integrate, don't invent a world

Unlike [[id:1161B5B0-3D67-4215-A6A0-093618B2FE3E][slovaris]] (a fully self-contained fictional universe), Acme
Bank is *not* meant to be an isolated world. It must fit into the
system's existing real-world reference data — specifically the GLEIF
LEI/counterparty universe already imported for tenants like Barclays
Plc — so that "provision GLEIF counterparties, then provision Acme"
just works, and Acme's trades can reference real-world institutions
(=DEUTDEFF=, =BARCUKGB=, etc.) as counterparties.

This means Acme's own legal entities need identifiers that are
*fake but well-formed*, not entities lifted wholesale from GLEIF (that
would be Barclays' job) and not an entirely disconnected naming
scheme either. Concretely:

- Acme's parties carry synthetic LEIs built on GLEIF's reserved
  pre-LOU test prefix =9695=, which GLEIF explicitly sets aside for
  non-production/test LEIs and will never assign to a real entity.
  Each is a real, checksum-valid (ISO 17442 / mod-97) 20-character
  code, stored the same way [[id:463F1441-0A6B-4F7D-B85C-985001B67A3B][How do I provision a party?]] stores real
  GLEIF LEIs — via =refdata_party_identifiers_tbl= with
  =coding_scheme_code = 'LEI'= — so nothing in the schema or UI needs
  to special-case a "fake" LEI.
- Acme's counterparties are the *real* GLEIF-imported counterparty
  set (the same one Barclays trades against), not synthetic
  counterparties — Acme is the one net-new entity, everything it
  interacts with is real.

* Relationship to the existing ores.synthetic generator

=ores.synthetic='s =organisation_generator_service= already generates,
deterministically from a seed, a party hierarchy, GB/US-locale
contacts (addresses, postcodes, phone formats), party/counterparty LEI
and BIC identifiers, and typed business units
(=DIVISION=/=BUSINESS_AREA=/=DESK=/=COST_CENTRE=), plus portfolios and
books — wired into shell provisioning today via =provision tenant
--source synthetic --seed 42= against a tenant named =acme=/"Acme
Corp" ([[proj:doc/recipes/shell/provisioning/acme_system_provision.org][How do I provision the system with Acme (synthetic)?]]). This
overlaps with a meaningful slice of what's proposed here, so it's
worth being explicit about why Acme Bank doesn't just reuse it:

- *Shape*: the existing generator builds a randomised tree from a
  seed; Acme Bank needs a specific, hand-authored org chart (fixed
  branches, fixed desks, fixed headcounts) that reads as a coherent,
  recognisable bank rather than an arbitrary structure.
- *Staff*: the existing generator stops at business units — it has no
  concept of accounts/staff sitting on a desk, which is the core new
  thing this story needs.
- *Locale*: GB/US contact generation exists; Hong Kong does not, and
  none of it covers *person* names (only company/address data).
- *LEIs*: the existing generator's LEI is =ctx.alphanumeric(20)= — a
  plain random 20-character string, not ISO 17442 mod-97 checksum-valid
  and not on GLEIF's =9695= test-prefix range. Acme Bank's identifiers
  need to be well-formed enough to look like real LEIs, which the
  current generator does not attempt.

Given that, the follow-up "build the generator" task should be scoped
as *extending* =ores.synthetic= (reusing its contact/LEI-storage
plumbing where it fits, fixing the LEI checksum gap, adding a
staff/account layer and HK locale) rather than a from-scratch
Python/Faker script, unless the fixed-shape requirement turns out to
fight the generator's random-tree design too much — that's a call for
the generator task itself to make once it's scoped in code, not this
analysis.

*Naming collision*: the existing synthetic-provisioning recipe already
claims the tenant code =acme= / name "Acme Corp" for its generic,
randomised smoke-test tenant. Acme Bank is a different, deliberately
curated entity and should not collide with it — the follow-up task
should provision it under a distinct tenant code (e.g. =acmebank= /
"Acme Bank Group plc"), leaving the existing =acme= synthetic tenant
untouched for its current purpose.

* Reference structure: Barclays Group

Barclays plc is a UK-headquartered universal bank organised (per its
public group structure and annual report) around two principal
divisions under a single group holding company:

- *Barclays UK (BUK)* — UK retail/consumer/business banking. Not a
  derivatives-trading division; excluded from the desk-level modelling
  below.
- *Barclays International (BI)* — split into:
  - *Corporate & Investment Bank (CIB)* — the derivatives-trading
    engine: Markets (FX, Rates, Credit, Equities), Banking, and
    Fixed Income/Global Markets. This is the division Acme Bank's
    trading-floor structure is modelled on.
  - *Consumer, Cards & Payments (CCP)*
- Legal entities of note: *Barclays Bank PLC* (the main international
  operating entity, home of most trading booking) and *Barclays Bank
  UK PLC* (ring-fenced retail entity, post-2018 structural reform).
- Major trading hubs / business centres: London (=GBLO=), New York
  (=USNY=), Hong Kong (=HKHK=), and other secondary centres (Frankfurt,
  Singapore, Tokyo) not modelled here.

For Acme Bank we take *CIB Markets* as the reference shape: a global
markets division split into asset-class business areas (Rates, Credit,
FX), with desks across three follow-the-sun centres, plus the Middle
Office/Market Risk support functions those desks depend on — the
pattern documented in [[id:7A3F2C91-B4E8-4D12-9F6A-1C8D5E302A74][Trading Structure: Party, Book, Portfolio, Business
Unit, Business Centre]].

* Proposed Acme Bank party hierarchy

#+begin_example
Acme Bank Group plc                LEI 9695ACMEBANKGRP00119   (party, root, system)
  └─ Acme Bank International plc   LEI 9695ACMEBANKINTL0140   (party, operational — CIB analogue)
       ├─ Acme Bank London Branch     LEI 9695ACMEBANKLON00163  (party — booking entity, GBLO)
       ├─ Acme Bank New York Branch   LEI 9695ACMEBANKNYC00131  (party — booking entity, USNY)
       └─ Acme Bank Hong Kong Branch  LEI TBD                    (party — booking entity, HKHK)
#+end_example

All LEIs use GLEIF's reserved =9695= pre-LOU test prefix and are
checksum-valid (ISO 17442 / mod-97) — see the design-principle section
above; the Hong Kong LEI is generated the same way when the branch is
built. Mapped onto =ores_refdata_parties_tbl=: a =system=-type root
party for the group, with =operational= children for the
international arm and its regional booking branches, each carrying
its LEI in =refdata_party_identifiers_tbl= the same way GLEIF-sourced
parties do — the same pattern used for Barclays Plc/GLEIF today, just
with a shallower, hand-curated tree instead of importing the full
GLEIF LEI hierarchy.

Each branch also needs *well-populated contact information* —
registered address, main switchboard number, compliance/legal contact
— stored via the existing party contact-information support, using
each region's real-world address conventions (postcode format, phone
country code) rather than placeholder text.

* Proposed trading floor and support functions

Per branch (London, New York, Hong Kong), the trading floor and its
supporting functions:

#+begin_example
Party: Acme Bank <Branch> plc
  └─ [DIVISION] Global Markets
       ├─ [BUSINESS_AREA] Rates
       │    └─ [DESK] IR Swaps <Branch>
       ├─ [BUSINESS_AREA] Credit
       │    └─ [DESK] Credit Trading <Branch>
       ├─ [BUSINESS_AREA] FX
       │    └─ [DESK] FX Rates <Branch>
       └─ [BUSINESS_AREA] Support Functions
            ├─ [COST_CENTRE] Middle Office <Branch>
            └─ [COST_CENTRE] Market Risk <Branch>
#+end_example

Nine =DESK=/=COST_CENTRE=-type business units total (three trading
desks + two support functions, per branch × three branches), each
carrying the branch's =business_centre_code= (=GBLO=/=USNY=/=HKHK=).
Business unit/portfolio/book *rows* are deferred per the scoping
decision above — this is the target shape the eventual seeding task
builds against.

* Proposed staff roster

Per branch, a modestly-sized trading floor plus support desks — not
the "biggest bank," but enough headcount to exercise RBAC across
front, middle, and risk:

| Function              | Roles (per branch)                          | Headcount |
|------------------------+----------------------------------------------+-----------|
| IR Swaps desk          | 1 Desk Head, 2 Traders                       |         3 |
| Credit Trading desk    | 1 Desk Head, 2 Traders                       |         3 |
| FX Rates desk          | 1 Desk Head, 2 Traders                       |         3 |
| Middle Office          | 1 Manager, 2 Analysts                        |         3 |
| Market Risk            | 1 Manager, 2 Analysts                        |         3 |
|------------------------+----------------------------------------------+-----------|
| *Total per branch*     |                                              |      *15* |

Plus, at the International-arm level (not branch-scoped): one Global
Markets division head and one head per business area (Rates, Credit,
FX) — 4 more accounts. *Grand total: 3 × 15 + 4 = 49 staff accounts.*

Each account's name is generated using the *branch's locale* — British
names for London, American names for New York, and Hong
Kong/Cantonese-appropriate names for Hong Kong — not a single Western
default across all three. Each account's profile picture is drawn
from [[proj:external/facestudio/manifest.json][FaceStudio Face Dataset]] (=external/facestudio/faces=, 377
images already committed), matched loosely to the generated name's
apparent gender and the branch's predominant ethnicity bucket (e.g.
=east_asian= for Hong Kong, =european=/=african=/=south_asian=/etc.
mix for London and New York, reflecting real-world diversity rather
than a monoculture per office).

* Gaps

- *No locale-aware name generation.* Producing realistic staff names
  per business centre (British names for London, Hong
  Kong/Cantonese-appropriate names for Hong Kong, etc.) needs a
  Python-side generator (Faker locales, or an equivalent), not a DB
  entity — this is a build-time data-generation gap, not a schema gap.
- *No account profile-picture support.* =ores_iam_accounts_tbl= has no
  image reference, and the only existing image table
  (=ores_assets_images_tbl=) stores SVG =svg_data=, not binary/JPEG —
  it was built for country flags, not photos. Decision: generalise
  =ores_assets_images_tbl= to a binary-agnostic asset blob (rather than
  add a parallel face-images table), plus an =image_id= FK on accounts.
  This touches every existing SVG consumer (flags, etc.) to migrate
  onto the generalised column — schema design work, not just data.
- *Only 1 business centre seeded.* =GBLO=/=USNY=/=HKHK= are standard
  FpML codes but the =business_centre= table only has one row
  populated in this environment today; London/New York/Hong Kong need
  seeding (small, low-risk — reuses the existing entity, no schema
  change).
- *No DQ artefact type for "generated organisation/staff data."* Every
  existing dataset in =ores_dq_datasets_tbl= backs a concrete
  reference-data table (countries, currencies, business units, …); an
  Acme-generated party tree and staff roster need their own artefact
  type(s) registered so they're trackable in the Data Librarian the
  same way, rather than being invisible generated data.
- *RBAC roles don't reach desk granularity.* Roles are tenant/party
  scoped (see [[id:3EC089EE-685B-469C-BF15-077951050248][Accounts and Roles]]); there's no "desk trader" or "desk
  head" role tied to a specific =business_unit=. Not a blocker for
  this task's scope (accounts can still exist and log in), but worth
  tracking for the RBAC exercise the parent story cares about.

None of these gaps block *starting* implementation — party/LEI/contact
population and business-centre seeding are pure data work with no
schema change. Account pictures and the DQ artefact type are schema
work and should be scoped as their own follow-up task(s) before the
staff-generation script can be considered complete end-to-end.

* Generation approach

A generator driven by a hand-authored, hardcoded company-structure
definition (the branches/desks/headcounts table above), rather than a
purely synthetic/randomised org chart — see the previous section for
why this likely means *extending* =ores.synthetic= rather than a
from-scratch Python/Faker script, pending that task's own scoping:

- The *shape* (which branches, which desks, how many people per desk)
  is fixed data — a human decided it's "sensible" — layered on top of
  whichever generator ends up producing it.
- For each slot in that shape, the generator *generates* the filler
  data: name (locale-matched to the branch, including Hong Kong —
  net-new locale work regardless of which generator is extended),
  email, a plausible employee-style username, a checksum-valid LEI on
  the =9695= test prefix, and a =faces/= image path chosen by matching
  the branch's ethnicity mix and the generated name's apparent gender.
- Output should follow the same shape as other =ores.seeder= dataset
  folders (manifest/model/populate-style JSON) so it plugs into the
  standard seeder pipeline once the account-picture and DQ
  artefact-type gaps are closed — not a bespoke one-off script whose
  output has to be hand-wired in.

This generator work, and the schema work it depends on, are follow-up
tasks under the parent story, not part of this analysis task.
