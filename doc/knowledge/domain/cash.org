* Summary

Cash narrows [[id:3407B8DF-D446-4658-8B5D-3368FFA44AD8][currency]] down further still: in its everyday sense it is
physical banknotes and coins, but the bookkeeping and finance sense
this note follows is broader -- the class of current [[id:AC6DAC84-8BFA-4E3A-BEF9-D747D920E7AB][assets]]
comprising currency or currency equivalents accessible immediately or
near-immediately, which includes highly [[id:0F727383-5B40-4366-91EF-36319F215997][liquid]] financial assets such
as bank deposits alongside physical notes -- the standard accounting
category of "cash and cash equivalents" (see [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash_and_cash_equivalents][Wikipedia: Cash and cash
equivalents]]).

* Detail

** Cash as the liquid end of a currency

Cash is money in its most liquid form for a given currency: physical
notes and coins, and -- in the financial rather than purely
physical sense -- any currency-equivalent asset that converts to
physical currency immediately or near-immediately, such as an
on-demand bank deposit. This is the narrowest of the concepts
[[id:3407B8DF-D446-4658-8B5D-3368FFA44AD8][Currency]]'s foundation rests on: not all money is a currency in the
standardised, systemic sense, and not all currency holdings are cash,
since a currency can equally be held in a comparatively illiquid
financial-asset form.

** Cash is an asset

Cash is, trivially, itself an [[id:AC6DAC84-8BFA-4E3A-BEF9-D747D920E7AB][asset]] -- the maximally liquid one, the
point every other asset's [[id:0F727383-5B40-4366-91EF-36319F215997][liquidity]] is ultimately measured against.
Framing cash this way is what makes "liquidity" a coherent, comparable
spectrum rather than a vague qualitative label: an asset's liquidity
is, precisely, how close it sits to behaving like cash itself.
